Analyzing posts to /r/astrophotography

Posted on Sun 29 April 2018 in posts • Tagged with python, jupyter notebook, astrophotography, eda, nlp, natural language processing

One of my hobbies is taking photographs of astronomical objects. The most stunning images combine operating the equipment under a dark sky with skillful processing using a variety of software. In fact, one rule of thumb in the astrophotography community is that for every hour you spend working with the equipment (driving to your site, setting everything up, capturing the hours long exposures, and so on), you should spend at least one hour processing the image. However, for the level of importance that processing has, there is very little data about what’s commonly used. I’d like to explore the processing software and techniques in the software that people use. What software do people commonly use for different types of astronomical object? What processing do they perform in that software? What processing steps are often used by images that are well received by the community?

MNIST with TensorFlow

Posted on Sat 24 March 2018 in posts • Tagged with python, jupyter notebook, machine learning, mnist, tensorflow, neural network

In my previous post I used a number of different classifiers from Scikit-Learn to attempt to classify images from the MNIST dataset. The best choice of those that I tried was a random forest, which offered 97.3% accuracy on the validation set. However, I did not use any sort of neural network. Because of the complexity of even simple image recognition tasks such as this one, neural networks work really well. This is because in some sense they learn the features that are most useful for classification. Intuitively, we can describe some of these features. For example straight lines appear in 1, 4, and 7, or a closed loop means that it's a 6, 8, 9, or 0. However, it would be very difficult to turn these into features that we could feed into one of the simple models we used before!
