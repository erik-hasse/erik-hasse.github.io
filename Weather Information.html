I. Preflight Preparation

C. Weather Information

Objective

To determine that the applicant exhibits satisfactory knowledge, risk management, and skills associated with weather information for a flight under VFR.

Knowledge

  1. Sources of weather data (e.g. National Weather Service, Flight Service) for flight planning purposes.
    • Weather briefing:
      • 1800wxbrief.com, 1-800-WXBRIEF
      • Types:
        • Standard - planning a flight with no previous briefing or weather review
        • Abbreviated - supplement previous briefing or other data
        • Outlook - time of deparature is 6+ hours away
        • Inflight - update a preflight briefing
      • Contents:
        • Adverse conditions
        • VFR not recommended
        • Synopsis
        • Current conditions
        • En route conditions
        • Destination forecast
        • Winds aloft
        • NOTAMs
        • ATC delay
        • On request:
          • Airspace status (Special use areas)
          • Printed NOTAM publication
          • Density altitude data
          • Air traffic services & rules
          • Customs/immigrations procedures
          • ADIZ rules
          • Search and rescue
          • Runway friction NOTAMs
          • GPS RAIM
          • Other assistance
    • Other sources:
      • Private industry
      • Flight Information Services (FIS-B via ADS-B)
      • Aviation weather cameras (Alaska)
      • Sources other than FAA, NWS, or their contractors may not be high quality
  2. Acceptable weather products and resources required for preflight planning, current and forecast weather for departure, en route, and arrival phases of flight.
  3. Meteorology applicable to the departure, en route, alternate, and destination under VFR in Visual Meteorological Conditions (VMC) to include expected climage and hazardous conditions such as:
    1. Atmospheric composition and stability
      • Standard temperature and pressure
        • Standard temperature is 59 deg F or 15 deg C
        • Standard pressure is 29.92" Hg or 1013.2 millibars
        • Pressure decreases by 1" Hg per 1000 feet
      • Stability
        • Stability is the ability of the atmosphere to resist upward motion
        • Stable air means stratiform clouds, low turbulence, steady precipitation, and poor visibility
        • Unstable air means cumuliform clouds, high turbulence, showery precipitation, and good visibility
    2. Wind (e.g. crosswind, tailwind, windshear, mountain wave, etc.)
      • Strong winds expected in large pressure gradiants
      • Wind shear:
        • High rate of change in wind velocity
        • May occur anywhere, but commonly:
          • Low-level inversion
          • Frontal zone/thunderstorm
          • Clear air turbulence from jet stream or circulation
        • Causes rapid changes in airflow around the plane, most hazardous at low altitudes
        • Can be found in:
          • Terminal forecasts
          • METARs
          • SIGMETS
          • LLWAS - Low level windshear alert system
          • PIREPs
    3. Temperature
      • Temperature inversion
        • Layer where temp is steady or increasing
        • Potential source of icing
        • Ground inversion causes poor visibility
        • Stable air, little or no turbulence
    4. Moisture/precipitation
      • More precipitation means thicker clouds. Significant precip is around 4000 feet thick clouds
    5. Weather system formation, including air masses and fronts
      • Pressure systems:
        • High
          • Inward, upward, and counter-clockwise
          • Rising air, cloudiness, precipitation, bad weather
          • Ridge is a line of high pressure
        • Low
          • Outward, downward, and clockwise
          • Descending air, dissipating clouds, good weather
          • Trough is a line of low pressure
      • Fronts:
        • Cold front
          • Cold, dense, stable air replaces warmer air
          • Cumulous, heavy rain, thunderstorms, hail, tornadoes, poor visibility, winds variable and gusting, temp/dew point/pressure drop rapidly
        • Warm front
          • Warm air flows over colder air
          • Stratiform clouds, drizzle, low ceilings/visibility, variable winds, temp rises
        • Stationary front
          • boundary between warm and cold air doesn't move, weather a mix of warm and cold fronts
        • Occluded front
          • fast moving cold front catches up to a slow warm front
    6. Clouds
    7. Turbulence
    8. Thunderstorms and microbursts
      • Requirements:
        • Water vapor
        • Unstable lapse rate
        • Lifting action
      • Stages:
        • Cumulus - Updrafts cause increasing raindrop size
        • Mature - Rain at surface, lightning, roll clouds
        • Dissipating - Downdrafts and dissipating rain
    9. Icing and freezing level information
      • Icing conditions:
        • Location of fronts
        • Cloud layers
        • Freezing level - lowest altitude where air temp reaches 0 deg C
        • Air temp and pressure - tends to be found in low pressure areas at or around freezing
      • Visible moisture and freezing temps are required for icing
      • Types of icing:
        • Structural - on surfaces on components
          • Clear ice - smooth, liquid drop spreads and gradually freezes
          • Rime ice - rough, small drops freeze rapidly
          • Mixed - very rough, when droops vary in size or mixed with ice/snow
        • Induction - in the engine induction system
      • Action: leave the area of visible icing - above/below clouds, turning
    10. Fog/mist
      • Formed when temperature and dewpoint are nearly identical (air cools or moisture is added)
      • Types:
        • Radiation - ground cools adjacent air - calm, clear nights
        • Advection - warm, humid air over a cold surface - coastal areas in the winter
        • Upslope - moist air moves up a slope
        • Frontal
        • Steam
    11. Frost
      • Causes roughness and surface friction -> loss of lift
    12. Obstructions to visibility (e.g. smoke, haze, volcanic ash, etc.)
  4. Flight deck displays of digital weather and aeronautical information.
    • FIS-B
      • Provided via ADS-B in
      • Requires ground station line of sight
      • As much as 15 minutes old, so not appropriate for avoiding thunderstorms
    • Other en-route weather info:
      • FSS on 122.2/RCO frequencies
      • ATIS along route of flight
      • ARTCC broadcasts
      • ATC (workload permitting)
  • Other:
    • METARs - Hourly, plus SPECI as needed
    • PIREPs - Pilot reports for actual conditions
    • TAFs - Terminal aerodrome forecasts
      • 5 mi from rwy complex center
      • 4 times/day
      • Usually either 24 or 30 hours
    • Aviation Area Forecast (FA) - Gulf of Mexico, Caribbean, Alaska
    • GFA - web graphics
    • AIRMET/SIGMET/Convective SIGMET
      • Sierra - IFR/Mtn Obscuration
      • Tango - Turbulence
      • Zulu - Icing/Freezing level
    • Center Weather Advisory
    • Winds aloft - use for best altitude, icing, turbulence/wind shear, temp inversions
    • Flight Categories:
      • LIFR - clg<500 ft, vis<1 mi
      • IFR - clg<1000 ft, vis<3 mi
      • MVFR - clg<3000 ft, vis<5 mi
      • VFR
  • Charts:
    • Surface analysis - fronts, boundries, sea level pressures
    • Ceiling and Visibility Analysis - Flight category
    • Prog charts - surface analysis over time, fronts, pressure, precip
    • Low level SIGWX - flight categories, turbulence, freezing levels
    • Convective outlook - probability of severe or non-severe convection
    • Constant pressure analysis
    • Freezing level graphics

Risk Management

  1. Factors involved in making the go/no-go and continue/divert decisions, to include:
    1. Circumstances that would make diversion prudent
    2. Personal weather minimums
    3. Hazardous weather conditions to include known or forecast icing or turbulence aloft
  2. Limitations of:
    1. Onboard weather equipment
    2. Aviation weather reports and forecasts
    3. Inflight weather resources

Skills

  1. Use available aviation weather resources to obtain an adequate weather briefing.
  2. Analyze the implications of at least three of the conditions listed in K3a through K3l above, using actual weather or weather conditions in a scenario provided by the evaluator.
  3. Correlate weather information to make a competent go/no-go decision.
